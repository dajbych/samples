High stakes for high-speed rail: Hong Kong trade body boss says over 100 subcontractors could suffer if no extra project funding

The Post learns small and medium-sized companies have little recourse to take legal action for compensation if the link is suspended

PUBLISHED : Thursday, 04 February, 2016, 8:23pm
UPDATED : Thursday, 04 February, 2016, 8:23pm

If the high-speed rail project linking Hong Kong with Guangzhou was abandoned because lawmakers fail to approve extra funding by mid-March, more than 100 subcontractors could be hurt and not just the 30-plus main contractors who stand to lose millions, the Post has found.

The head of a trade body said the subcontractors had little recourse, adding that most of these small and medium-sized firms could not afford to take legal action to seek compensation.

The concerns came after it was revealed that more than 100 major and minor construction contracts could be affected if construction of the 26-km Hong Kong section of the Guangzhou-Shenzhen-Hong Kong Express Rail Link was halted.

READ MORE: MTR chief warns of ‘catastrophic’ aftermath if Legco votes down high-speed rail after shareholders let MTR take on potential further cost overruns

The MTR Corporation, entrusted by the Hong Kong government with the design and construction of the cross-border project, said on Monday that the HK$65 billion budget approved by the Legislative Council in 2009 would be used up by mid-year. It said it needed to notify its building contractors by next month to allow time to make preparations, regardless of whether or not Legco approved an additional HK$19.6 billion.

MTR projects director Dr Philco Wong Nai-keung warned that, if the project was stopped, more than 40 major construction contracts and 60 minor ones would have to be suspended or cancelled, resulting in heaps of lawsuits seeking compensation.

In a submission to Legco’s public works subcommittee last month, the government made it known that 42 major contracts valued at about HK$45 billion were awarded to more than 30 local and foreign firms.

An MTR spokeswoman told the Post that main contractors had a legal right to claim compensation for breach of contract if the project was suspended or cancelled by the railway operator.

But she said the project was still proceeding as scheduled, adding that the MTR had ongoing communications with its building contractors.

Asked whether legal action would be brought against the MTR if their contracts were cancelled unilaterally, Leighton Asia, a main contractor, said it would not comment.

Lawyer Daniel Wong Kwok-tung noted that while the main contractors could sue the MTR for breach of contract, they might also face lawsuits from subcontractors over the same type of breach.

Wong said subcontractors were carrying out the construction work in accordance with the contracts they entered with specific main contractors and not directly with the MTR.

READ MORE: 7,000 jobs could be lost if Hong Kong high-speed rail link is suspended, says MTRC boss

“Unless the work suspension stems from irresistible force and unavoidable accidents such as natural disasters, the main contractors are held liable,” said Wong.

The lawyers said it was unlikely that the prolonged Legco debate about funding would be deemed an unforeseeable cause beyond reasonable human control.

But Lawrence Ng San-wa, president of Hong Kong Construction Sub-contractors Association, said subcontractors played a more passive role compared with many main contractors that had more financial resources to pursue legal action.

Depending on the terms of individual contracts, a dispute arising out of construction agreements would usually be settled by arbitration if subcontractors wanted to seek compensation from main contractors after the project was suspended, Ng said.

“It may take between 18 months and three years to settle it,” he said.

Ng said subcontractors, which as a rule were small and medium-sized local companies, just wanted to carry on with their work and could not afford the time or financial costs associated with a legal action.

“These subcontractors are in dire situations,” Ng said. According to industry practice, such companies had to continue to pay wages and advance the money to construction material vendors even if a fresh batch of funding had not been secured.

He said the trade body did not maintain statistics on the exact number of construction workers or firms involved in the rail link project but believed at least hundreds of companies were subcontracted some of its work.
