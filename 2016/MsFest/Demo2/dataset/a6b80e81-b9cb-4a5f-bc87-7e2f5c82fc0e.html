WestJet flight attendants reject tentative agreement

Amanda Stephenson, Calgary Herald
More from Amanda Stephenson, Calgary Herald
Published on: November 25, 2014 | Last Updated: November 25, 2014 6:28 AM MST
Share Adjust Comment Print

WestJet flight attendants have voted down a new tentative agreement aimed at quelling ongoing unionization efforts at the Calgary-based airline.

The agreement, the result of six months of negotiation between management and WestJet’s non-unionized flight attendant association, was rejected by 57 per cent of flight attendants who voted. Turnout was extremely strong, with 90 per cent of the airline’s 2,600 flight attendants casting a ballot.

Tyson Matheson — WestJet’s vice-president, Inflight — said he believes the terms of the agreement were fair and called the results of the vote “disappointing.” He said a survey will be circulated to flight attendants soon in an attempt to find out why the agreement was rejected.

Matheson added it’s possible a mediation or arbitration process could be required to get an agreement ratified, but said he’s still hopeful changes can be made at the negotiating table that will prove acceptable to both sides.

“I’m still very confident we’re going to get a ‘yes’ vote from our flight attendants,” he said.

While WestJet has long prided itself on its positive workplace culture, it’s clear some employees are disgruntled with some of the changes that have taken place over the last two years — a period of rapid growth and expansion at the airline.

One particularly contentious move was the establishment of new crew bases in Toronto and Vancouver, a departure from the old model where all pilots and flight attendants started their shifts in Calgary. The airline also moved to a new ratio of one flight attendant for every 50 passengers, a change from the old ratio of one to 40.

In recent months, both the Canadian Union of Public Employees and an in-house group calling itself the WestJet Professional Flight Attendants Association (WPFFA) have been trying to convince the airline’s flight attendants to sign membership cards.

In an emailed statement, a representative of the WPFFA said the results of the vote shouldn’t be seen as a negative for WestJet, since a 90 per cent turnout shows flight attendants are passionate about the future of the company.

But the representative said the rejection of the tentative agreement makes no difference to the WPFFA, because the group still believes flight attendants need the kind of legally binding and enforceable collective agreement that is only possible under a certified union.

“With this in mind we continue our drive for certification,” the representative stated.

WestJet acknowledged in October that the new agreement — pitched as a first-of-its kind document with more teeth than any previous memorandum of agreement — was an attempt to address employee concerns that might be fuelling the unionization drives.

The tentative agreement covered not just compensation, but also formally established a series of work rules and an enforceable dispute resolution process.

Matheson said Monday it is still “status quo” at the airline on the unionization front.

“Anytime there is a union drive or a threat toward us, we take those very seriously,” he said. “Employee relations has always been key to WestJet’s success, and even though we got a ‘no’ vote, this doesn’t change that.”

Ian Holloway, dean of law at the University of Calgary, said the significance of the flight attendant vote can’t be glossed over, considering WestJet’s history as a non-unionized company and the pride it has always taken in its relations with employees.

But Holloway added it may not be surprising, either, considering WestJet has grown from a small start-up to a major North American carrier and rival to Air Canada.

“Maybe what this tells us is it’s not always possible to be a ‘family’ company anymore, even though that’s the ethos they prided themselves on,” Holloway said. “Maybe this case shows us the limits of intimacy in the industrial relations setting.”

WestJet’s pilots are also set to vote soon on a new tentative agreement. It will be the airline’s second attempt at getting a pilots’ deal ratified after that employee group rejected a first offer last year.
