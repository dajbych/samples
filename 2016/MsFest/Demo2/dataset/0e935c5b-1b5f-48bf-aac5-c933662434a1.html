7 ways Wargaming is doing eSports right

Wargaming is onto a winning formula with its ever-evolving World of Tanks eSports tournaments.

By Nathan Lawrence on Dec 28 2015 10:17PM
0

I recently had the pleasure of attending the World of Tanks Pacific Rumble in Tokyo, which saw the best of the Asia-Pacific region taking on the best that North America had to offer. It was a fantastic tournament with some evenly balanced teams and some tight matches that came right down to the wire. Obviously, the comparable skill of the teams helps to facilitate this type of result, but it’s also the result of Wargaming’s willingness to mix things up—and sometimes drastically—to ensure that each successive World of Tanks tournament is better for contenders and viewers than what came before.

Here’s a breakdown of why I think Wargaming is doing eSports right.

Wargaming has a history of big changes
In previous tournaments, there have been complaints that there was no incentive for teams to try and score more than an early single-round win. Tournaments descended into audience-unfriendly stalemates as the first side to score a win then turtled to ensure victory by successive draws. Not anymore. Enter Wargaming’s solution: Attack/Defence mode. In this mode, teams rotate between offensive and defensive roles, as the defensive team has to manage its defensive line across two points of interest that can be secured by the attacking team. The risk, though, of rushing for a sneaky cap is that a single hit from a defending player resets the countdown.

Wargaming has big changes for the future
The opening match of the Pacific Rumble was an exhibition match to show off the new Tier X tournament structure, instead of the usual Tier VIII tournament restriction. Interestingly, the match was played out between eventual tournament finalists Noble eSports and EL Gaming. While EL went on to ultimately win the tournament, Noble won the Tier X exhibition: a sure sign of interesting things to come. Among the competing players, there were mixed thoughts about the imminent introduction of Tier X, but Wargaming is confident it will work, seeing it as an introduction that also acts as an incentive for the existing player base to work their way up to Tier X.

The boring stuff is done off camera
While this may not be different from certain real-world sports, the most boring stuff is done off camera and isn’t part of what the audience sees. A coin toss is used to determine who gets first choice of map elimination, and that goes back and forth until four maps are eliminated. Then the coin-toss winner selects the first map from the remaining two options, and the coin-toss loser selects the starting position. It’s important behind-the-scenes stuff, but Wargaming gets that the audience doesn’t have to see it.

Limited downtime between rounds
Despite the repetitive nature of the same intro video between every round, there’s not a whole lot of waiting between rounds. There are a few minutes as everything is reset, but then the teams are left with a short window to select their roster of tanks. Considering how similar the teams tended to be in the types of tanks they selected during the Pacific Rumble, it was good to see that this stage didn’t outstay its welcome.

World of Tanks has universal appeal
Strategy-heavy eSports games such as StarCraft II, LOL or DotA 2 are harder for audiences to get into because they assume a certain amount of requisite audience knowledge to make sense of what the hell is going on. At the other end of the spectrum, action-heavy eSports titles such as Counter-Strike or Call of Duty may be easier for audiences to understand in terms of the mechanics, but the higher-impact violence makes it a trickier sell for mainstream viewers. World of Tanks mixes the accessibility of a shooter with the strategy of a MOBA/RTS title, and because it’s tanks shooting tanks, there’s no need to worry about gore. It means that fans of World of Tanks, gamers in general, and even general audience members curious about eSports can watch a tournament and find enjoyment.

Wargaming is fostering tomorrow’s champions
During my interviews with key Wargaming eSports staff, we talked about how the company has introduced training incentives to help train and foster tomorrow’s eSports contenders. Wargaming has enlisted the aid of some of the best players to help create video content, and there are a number of smaller regional tournaments to give players a platform to showcase their skills. Apart from this, there are dedicated eSports staff across regions to help draw more players and viewers into the fold. Wargaming understands that eSports is important to World of Tanks, and it’s not afraid to inject resources into the ever-growing area.

It’s come a long way
Deputy head of global eSports Alexey Kuznetsov reminisced during our interview about how far World of Tanks has come in terms of eSports. He was heavily involved in setting up the original tournaments, with volunteer staff from Wargaming and everything tracked manually with pen and paper. Nowadays, there are multiple commentators, multiple-language stream options and high production values. On top of this, Wargaming understands that demand for eSports comes from the community, which means there’s a chance you may hear about World of Warships tournaments in the future.

As Wargaming continues to refine its approach to World of Tanks tournaments, it’s showcasing a fantastic level of confidence in making big changes, sometimes just to see how it will impact balance In terms of where World of Tanks started with eSports and where it is at now, if Wargaming can maintain its progressive trajectory, I see no reason why the tournaments won’t move from strength to strength.
